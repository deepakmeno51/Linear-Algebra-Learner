Why Each Topic Matters for Deep Learning
▾

Vectors & Dot Products

Every data point is a vector. Every neuron's output is a dot product \(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x} + b\). Cosine similarity of embeddings uses dot products. Attention scores are scaled dot products.

Matrix Multiplication

Every dense layer computes \(y = Wx\). Batched training computes \(Y = XW^T\). The entire forward pass is a sequence of matrix-vector products. Backprop uses transposes \(W^T\).

Eigenvalues

Eigenvalues of weight matrices control gradient flow. If \(|\lambda_{max}| > 1\), gradients explode. If \(|\lambda_{max}| < 1\), they vanish. Batch norm and gradient clipping fight these effects.

Covariance & PCA

Covariance matrices describe feature correlations. PCA removes redundancy before training. Batch normalization implicitly whitens feature distributions.

SVD & Low-Rank

SVD is the foundation of LoRA fine-tuning (constraining weight updates to low-rank matrices). Spectral normalization divides weights by \(\sigma_1\) for GAN stability. Truncated SVD powers recommender systems.

Orthogonality

Orthogonal weight initialization (QR decomposition) ensures signal preserves its norm at startup. Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization underlies many numerical methods in ML.

★
The Master Conceptual Thread
▾
🔗 Everything Connects

Here is the core idea that runs through all 10 modules:

A matrix \(A\) is a linear transformation. It stretches, rotates, and reflects vectors. The eigenvectors are the special directions that don't rotate — they only stretch, by factor \(\lambda\). For symmetric matrices (like covariance matrices), eigenvectors are orthogonal and form the perfect new coordinate system.

PCA finds the eigenvectors of the covariance matrix \(\Sigma = \frac{1}{m}X^TX\) — these are the directions of maximum variance. Project your data onto the top \(k\) eigenvectors, and you've kept the most information in fewer dimensions.

SVD extends this to any (rectangular) matrix. Every matrix \(A = U\Sigma V^T\) decomposes into a rotation \(V^T\), axis-aligned scaling \(\Sigma\), and another rotation \(U\). The singular values are \(\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i(A^TA)}\), connecting SVD back to eigenvalues.

The Big Picture Equation

-- Neural forward pass -- y = W_L · ReLU( W_{L-1} · ... · ReLU(W_1 · x) ... ) -- Each Wᵢ has an SVD: -- W_i = U_i · Σ_i · V_i^T -- So each layer = rotate input, scale each axis, rotate output -- -- Training learns the geometry (U, V) and importance (Σ) simultaneously --

Module 01

Vectors, Norms & Dot Products

The atomic objects of linear algebra — and of every neural network

A vector is the fundamental object in linear algebra. In deep learning, almost everything is a vector: an input data point, a weight vector, an activation, a gradient, an embedding. Before we can understand matrices, transformations, or decompositions, we must own vectors completely.

1.1
What Is a Vector? Geometric vs. Algebraic View
▾
📐 Definition — Vector in ℝⁿ

A vector \(\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n\) is an ordered list of \(n\) real numbers, written as a column:

\[\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n \end{bmatrix}\]

The integer \(n\) is the dimension of the vector.

There are two ways to think about a vector, and you must be fluent in both:

Algebraic View

A vector is a list of coordinates. \([3, -1, 2]^T\) in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) means "3 units in the first direction, −1 in the second, 2 in the third." This is what your code sees: an array of numbers.

Geometric View

A vector is an arrow from the origin to a point in space. Its length (magnitude) and direction are what matter geometrically. Two vectors with the same direction but different lengths are scalar multiples of each other.

🧠 Deep Learning — Every Layer Input Is a Vector

When you feed an image to a neural network, you first flatten it into a vector: a 28×28 image becomes a vector in \(\mathbb{R}^{784}\). A sentence represented with word embeddings is a sequence of vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^{512}\) (or whatever the embedding dim is). The weights of a single neuron also form a vector.

1.3
The Dot Product — The Most Important Operation
▾
📐 Definition — Dot Product (Inner Product)
\[\mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{x}^T \mathbf{y} = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i y_i\]

Geometrically: \(\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y} = \|\mathbf{x}\|_2 \|\mathbf{y}\|_2 \cos\theta\) where \(\theta\) is the angle between the vectors.

The geometric formula is the key: the dot product measures how aligned two vectors are. If \(\theta = 0°\), they're parallel, dot product = product of lengths. If \(\theta = 90°\), they're perpendicular, dot product = 0. This is why the dot product is the natural similarity measure.

📐 Definition — Orthogonality

Vectors \(\mathbf{x}\) and \(\mathbf{y}\) are orthogonal if \(\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y} = 0\). They point in completely independent directions — knowing the component along \(\mathbf{x}\) tells you nothing about the component along \(\mathbf{y}\).

📐 Definition — Cosine Similarity
\[\text{sim}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \frac{\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y}}{\|\mathbf{x}\|_2 \|\mathbf{y}\|_2} = \cos\theta \in [-1, 1]\]

Cosine similarity is the dot product of the normalized vectors. It measures angle (direction similarity) ignoring magnitude.

🧠 Deep Learning — Everything Is Dot Products

A neuron computes: \(\text{output} = \sigma(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x} + b)\). The core is \(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}\) — a dot product between weight vector \(\mathbf{w}\) and input \(\mathbf{x}\). A neuron fires strongly when \(\mathbf{w}\) and \(\mathbf{x}\) are aligned (similar direction). This is the geometric meaning of a neuron: it is a direction detector.

In transformers, attention scores are \(\text{score}(q, k) = q^T k / \sqrt{d_k}\) — a scaled dot product between query and key vectors. High score = high alignment = attend to that token.

Word embeddings: king − man + woman ≈ queen works because these linear operations on vectors correspond to meaningful geometric relationships.

1.4
Vector Operations & Linear Combinations
▾
📐 Definition — Linear Combination

A linear combination of vectors \(\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\) with scalars \(c_1, \ldots, c_k\) is:

\[c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_k\mathbf{v}_k\]

The set of all possible linear combinations is the span of those vectors.

📋 Example — Linear Combination in Context

In a neural network, the output of a layer is a linear combination of input features:

\[y = w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2 + \cdots + w_n x_n = \mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}\]

The weights \(w_i\) are the coefficients of the linear combination. The network learns which combination of input features is relevant.

Outer Product — Creating a Matrix from Two Vectors

The outer product \(\mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^T\) produces a matrix. If \(\mathbf{u} \in \mathbb{R}^m\) and \(\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n\), then \(\mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^T \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\) with entry \((i,j) = u_i v_j\). This is a rank-1 matrix — critical for SVD and attention mechanisms.

-- Outer product example -- u = [1, 2]ᵀ, v = [3, 0, -1]ᵀ uvᵀ = [1·3 1·0 1·(-1)] = [ 3 0 -1 ] [2·3 2·0 2·(-1)] [ 6 0 -2 ] -- This is a rank-1 matrix. Every column is a multiple of u. -- -- SVD writes any matrix as sum of rank-1 outer products: A = Σ σᵢ uᵢvᵢᵀ --

1.5
Vector Projection — The Foundation of PCA
▾
📐 Definition — Projection of x onto unit vector u
\[\text{proj}_{\mathbf{u}} \mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{x})\, \mathbf{u}\]

The scalar \(\alpha = \mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{x}\) is the coordinate (how far along \(\mathbf{u}\) the projection reaches). The vector \(\alpha\,\mathbf{u}\) is the projected vector. If \(\mathbf{u}\) is not unit, divide: \(\text{proj}_\mathbf{u}\mathbf{x} = \frac{\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{x}}{\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{u}}\mathbf{u}\).

🧠 Why Projection Is the Heart of PCA

In PCA, we project each data point \(\mathbf{x}_i\) onto a direction \(\mathbf{u}\) (a principal component). The scalar coordinate \(\alpha_i = \mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{x}_i\) is the compressed representation. PCA chooses \(\mathbf{u}\) to maximize the variance of these coordinates across the dataset. The key formula: variance along direction \(\mathbf{u}\) = \(\mathbf{u}^T \Sigma \mathbf{u}\), where \(\Sigma\) is the covariance matrix.

📋 Worked Example

Project \(\mathbf{x} = [3, 3]^T\) onto \(\mathbf{u} = [1/\sqrt{2},\, 1/\sqrt{2}]^T\) (the diagonal direction):

\[\alpha = \mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{x} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\cdot 3 + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\cdot 3 = \frac{6}{\sqrt{2}} = 3\sqrt{2}\] \[\text{proj}_\mathbf{u}\mathbf{x} = 3\sqrt{2} \cdot \begin{bmatrix}1/\sqrt{2}\\1/\sqrt{2}\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}3\\3\end{bmatrix}\]

\(\mathbf{x}\) lies exactly on the diagonal — zero reconstruction error. This is why the diagonal is the first principal component when data lies along it!

Module 02

Matrices & Linear Maps

How matrices encode transformations — and what they do geometrically

A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers, but more importantly, it is a linear transformation between vector spaces. Every dense layer in a neural network is a matrix. Understanding what matrices do geometrically — before we worry about how to decompose them — is critical.

2.1
Matrix Basics — Shape, Notation, Types
▾
📐 Definition — Matrix

A matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) has \(m\) rows and \(n\) columns. It represents a linear map \(A: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m\). Entry \(A_{ij}\) is in row \(i\), column \(j\).

Orthogonal \(Q\)\(Q^TQ = I\), so \(Q^{-1}=Q^T\)Preserves lengths and angles (rotation)

📝 Why Symmetric Matrices Dominate Deep Learning

Covariance matrices \(\Sigma = \frac{1}{m}X^TX\) are always symmetric (and PSD). Gram matrices \(G = XX^T\) are symmetric. Hessians \(H = \nabla^2 \mathcal{L}\) of loss functions are symmetric. The reason symmetric matrices are so special: they always have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors — the Spectral Theorem (Module 06).

2.2
Matrix Multiplication — Four Interpretations
▾

Matrix multiplication \(C = AB\) where \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times k}\), \(B \in \mathbb{R}^{k\times n}\) gives \(C \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\). There are four equivalent ways to see it — each illuminating in different contexts.

Row-Column (Standard)

\(C_{ij} = \sum_{r=1}^k A_{ir} B_{rj}\) = dot product of row \(i\) of \(A\) with column \(j\) of \(B\). Complexity: \(O(mnk)\).

Column View

Each column of \(C\) is \(A\) times the corresponding column of \(B\): column \(j\) of \(C\) = \(A\) × (column \(j\) of \(B\)). Think: \(C\) applies \(A\) to each column of \(B\).

Row View

Each row of \(C\) is the corresponding row of \(A\) times \(B\). Think: row \(i\) of \(C\) = (row \(i\) of \(A\)) × \(B\).

Sum of Rank-1 Matrices

\(AB = \sum_{r=1}^k (\text{col}_r A)(\text{row}_r B)\). Expresses \(AB\) as a sum of \(k\) outer products. Critical for understanding SVD.

⚠️ Non-Commutativity

Matrix multiplication is not commutative: \(AB \neq BA\) in general. The order of transformations matters. In deep learning, the order of layers matters. Backpropagation uses the chain rule with transposes: \(\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial W} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial y} \cdot x^T\), and the order matters for shapes to work out.

Key Rules

-- Transpose rules (used constantly in backprop) -- (AB)ᵀ = BᵀAᵀ -- Order reverses! (ABC)ᵀ = CᵀBᵀAᵀ (A + B)ᵀ = Aᵀ + Bᵀ -- Inverse rules -- (AB)⁻¹ = B⁻¹A⁻¹ -- Order reverses! (Aᵀ)⁻¹ = (A⁻¹)ᵀ -- For orthogonal Q: Q⁻¹ = Qᵀ (cheapest inverse possible) --

2.4
Rank, Determinant & Invertibility
▾
📐 Definition — Rank

The rank of a matrix \(A\) is the dimension of its column space (= dimension of its row space). Equivalently, it's the number of linearly independent columns (or rows). For \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\), \(\text{rank}(A) \leq \min(m, n)\).

Full Rank

\(\text{rank}(A) = \min(m,n)\). The matrix doesn't "lose information" — every output dimension is reachable. An invertible square matrix has full rank.

Rank Deficient

\(\text{rank}(A) < \min(m,n)\). Some input dimensions map to zero. The null space is nontrivial. The matrix "squashes" the input into a lower-dimensional subspace.

📐 Definition — Determinant

For a square matrix \(A\), the determinant \(\det(A)\) measures the signed volume scaling factor of the transformation. Key facts:

  • \(\det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B)\)
  • \(\det(A^T) = \det(A)\)
  • \(\det(A) = 0\) iff \(A\) is singular (rank-deficient)
  • For a 2×2: \(\det\begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix} = ad - bc\)
  • \(\det(A) = \prod_i \lambda_i\) (product of eigenvalues!)
🧠 Rank in Deep Learning — Expressivity

A weight matrix with rank \(r\) can only map inputs to an \(r\)-dimensional subspace, regardless of the input dimension. If \(r \ll n\), the layer is a bottleneck. LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) deliberately constrains weight updates to be rank-\(r\) matrices, dramatically reducing trainable parameters while preserving most of the model's capability.

Module 03

Vector Spaces, Bases & Subspaces

The geometric arena — what "space" a matrix operates in

3.1
Vector Spaces — What Makes a Space
▾
📐 Definition — Vector Space

A vector space over \(\mathbb{R}\) is a set \(V\) with two operations (addition, scalar multiplication) satisfying 8 axioms. The most important ones for intuition:

  • \(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} \in V\) (closed under addition)
  • \(c\mathbf{v} \in V\) (closed under scalar multiplication)
  • The zero vector \(\mathbf{0} \in V\)

Key: \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is the archetypal vector space. But function spaces, polynomial spaces, etc. are also vector spaces.

📐 Definition — Subspace

A subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is a subset \(S \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n\) that is itself a vector space — it must contain \(\mathbf{0}\), be closed under addition, and be closed under scalar multiplication.

Examples: a line through the origin, a plane through the origin, all of \(\mathbb{R}^n\), just \{\mathbf{0}\}.

⚠️ A Subspace Must Contain the Origin

The set of all vectors with \(x_1 + x_2 = 1\) is NOT a subspace — it doesn't contain \(\mathbf{0}\). The set with \(x_1 + x_2 = 0\) IS a subspace. This distinction matters when thinking about affine transforms (neural network layers with bias: \(y = Wx + b\) is an affine map, not a linear map unless \(b = 0\)).

3.2
The Four Fundamental Subspaces Strang §3
▾

Every matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\) with rank \(r\) defines four fundamental subspaces. Together they tell you everything about what \(Ax = b\) can and cannot do.

Column Space \(C(A)\)

All possible outputs: \(\{A\mathbf{x} : \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n\}\). Lives in \(\mathbb{R}^m\). Dimension = \(r\). The equation \(A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}\) has a solution iff \(\mathbf{b} \in C(A)\).

Null Space \(N(A)\)

All inputs that produce zero output: \(\{\mathbf{x}: A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{0}\}\). Lives in \(\mathbb{R}^n\). Dimension = \(n-r\). Orthogonal complement of the row space.

Row Space \(C(A^T)\)

Directions that survive the transform. Lives in \(\mathbb{R}^n\). Dimension = \(r\). Orthogonal complement of the null space. Every \(\mathbf{x}\) = (row space part) + (null space part).

Left Null Space \(N(A^T)\)

All outputs that can't be reached: \(\{\mathbf{y}: A^T\mathbf{y}=\mathbf{0}\}\). Lives in \(\mathbb{R}^m\). Dimension = \(m-r\). Orthogonal complement of column space.

🏛 Rank-Nullity Theorem
\[\text{rank}(A) + \text{nullity}(A) = n\]

where nullity = \(\dim N(A) = n - r\). Every input vector decomposes uniquely into a row-space component (which produces output) and a null-space component (which vanishes).

🏛 Fundamental Theorem of Linear Algebra (Strang)

The row space and null space are orthogonal complements in \(\mathbb{R}^n\). The column space and left null space are orthogonal complements in \(\mathbb{R}^m\):

\[C(A^T) \perp N(A) \quad \text{in } \mathbb{R}^n\] \[C(A) \perp N(A^T) \quad \text{in } \mathbb{R}^m\]
🧠 Subspaces in Neural Networks

A weight matrix \(W \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{out}\times d_{in}}\) with rank \(r < d_{in}\) has a nontrivial null space of dimension \(d_{in}-r\). Any input in that null space produces zero output from that layer — it's invisible to the network. This is why rank matters for expressivity. If \(W\) has rank 10 but \(d_{in} = 512\), then 502 directions in input space are completely ignored by that layer.

3.3
Linear Independence, Basis & Dimension
▾
📐 Definition — Linear Independence

Vectors \(\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\) are linearly independent if the only solution to:

\[c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_k\mathbf{v}_k = \mathbf{0}\]

is \(c_1 = c_2 = \cdots = c_k = 0\). Otherwise, they're linearly dependent — at least one is redundant.

📐 Definition — Basis

A basis for a vector space \(V\) is a set of vectors that is both (1) linearly independent and (2) spans \(V\). Every vector in \(V\) has a unique representation as a linear combination of basis vectors. The number of basis vectors = \(\dim(V)\).

📐 Definition — Orthonormal Basis

A basis \(\{\mathbf{u}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{u}_n\}\) is orthonormal if \(\mathbf{u}_i^T\mathbf{u}_j = \delta_{ij}\) (0 if \(i\neq j\), 1 if \(i=j\)). Finding coordinates in an orthonormal basis is trivial:

\[\mathbf{z} = \sum_{i=1}^n (\mathbf{u}_i^T\mathbf{z})\,\mathbf{u}_i \qquad \text{(just take dot products!)}\]

This is the key advantage of orthonormal bases. For a general basis, finding coordinates requires solving a linear system — \(O(n^3)\). For an orthonormal basis, each coordinate is just a dot product — \(O(n)\) each, \(O(n^2)\) total. Eigenvectors of symmetric matrices always form orthonormal bases (Spectral Theorem), which is why they're the perfect basis choice.

📋 Change of Basis Example

Suppose the eigenvectors of \(A = \begin{bmatrix}1&2\\2&1\end{bmatrix}\) are \(\mathbf{u}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}[1,1]^T\) and \(\mathbf{u}_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}[-1,1]^T\).

Any vector \(\mathbf{z} = [a,b]^T\) in the eigenbasis:

\[\alpha_1 = \mathbf{u}_1^T\mathbf{z} = \frac{a+b}{\sqrt{2}}, \qquad \alpha_2 = \mathbf{u}_2^T\mathbf{z} = \frac{-a+b}{\sqrt{2}}\]

Then \(A\mathbf{z} = \lambda_1\alpha_1\mathbf{u}_1 + \lambda_2\alpha_2\mathbf{u}_2 = 3\alpha_1\mathbf{u}_1 + (-1)\alpha_2\mathbf{u}_2\). Matrix multiplication becomes scalar multiplication in the eigenbasis!

3.4
Markov Chains & Steady States — A DL Motivation
▾

This is where the lecture (Slides 6–10) motivates eigenvalues beautifully. Consider two restaurants — Chinese and Mexican — where students switch between them each day with probabilities \(p\) and \(q\).

📐 Setup — Markov Transition Matrix
\[\mathbf{v}_{(t+1)} = M\,\mathbf{v}_{(t)}, \quad M = \begin{bmatrix}1-p & q \\ p & 1-q\end{bmatrix}\]

The sequence \(\mathbf{v}_{(0)}, M\mathbf{v}_{(0)}, M^2\mathbf{v}_{(0)}, \ldots\) — does it converge?

Yes! After enough steps, \(M^n\mathbf{v}_{(0)} \to k\,\mathbf{e}_d\) where \(\mathbf{e}_d\) is the dominant eigenvector of \(M\) (eigenvalue 1). This is the steady state. The key insight from the lecture (Slide 10):

-- Sequence behaviour depends ENTIRELY on dominant eigenvalue λ_d: -- |λ_d| > 1 → sequence EXPLODES (diverges) |λ_d| < 1 → sequence VANISHES (collapses to 0) |λ_d| = 1 → sequence CONVERGES to steady state This is exactly the vanishing/exploding gradient problem in RNNs! -- In an RNN: h_t = tanh(Wh_{t-1} + Ux_t) -- -- Gradients flow back as products of W, controlled by its eigenvalues --
🧠 RNNs and Eigenvalue Control

In a vanilla RNN, the gradient of the loss w.r.t. the hidden state at time \(t=0\) involves \(W^T\) multiplied many times. If \(|\lambda_{\max}(W)| > 1\), gradients explode. If \(< 1\), they vanish. This is why LSTMs and GRUs use gating mechanisms — they adaptively control the effective eigenvalue, keeping it near 1 when needed.

Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors

The special vectors that refuse to stray from their path under transformation

4.1
Core Definition & Geometric Intuition
▾
📐 Definition — Eigenvector & Eigenvalue

For square matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}\), a nonzero vector \(\mathbf{x}\) is an eigenvector with eigenvalue \(\lambda\) if:

\[A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}\]

The direction of \(\mathbf{x}\) is unchanged by \(A\). Only the magnitude changes — scaled by \(\lambda\).

Why are these special? For any other vector, matrix multiplication changes both direction and length. Eigenvectors are the coordinate axes where \(A\) acts diagonally. They're the "natural coordinate system" of the transformation.

📋 Concrete Example — IIT Lecture Slide 4

For \(A = \begin{bmatrix}1&2\\2&1\end{bmatrix}\):

-- Random vector: direction changes -- A·[1,3]ᵀ = [7,5]ᵀ ← completely different direction -- Eigenvector: only scales -- A·[1,1]ᵀ = [3,3]ᵀ = 3·[1,1]ᵀ ← λ₁=3, scaled along diagonal A·[-1,1]ᵀ = [1,-1]ᵀ = -1·[-1,1]ᵀ ← λ₂=-1, reflected along anti-diagonal

The eigenvectors point along the axes of the transformation. One direction is stretched by 3, the other is reflected.

\(\lambda > 1\)
Stretched in that direction
\(0 < \lambda < 1\)
Compressed toward origin
\(\lambda < 0\)
Reflected + scaled
4.2
How to Find Eigenvalues — The Characteristic Polynomial
▾

The equation \(A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}\) can be rewritten as \((A - \lambda I)\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}\). For a nonzero solution to exist, the matrix \((A - \lambda I)\) must be singular. Singular means determinant = 0.

📐 The Characteristic Equation
\[\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\]

This is a polynomial of degree \(n\) in \(\lambda\). Its roots are the eigenvalues. For an \(n\times n\) matrix, there are always exactly \(n\) eigenvalues (counting multiplicity, allowing complex values).

1
Compute \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\)

This gives the characteristic polynomial. For 2×2: \(\lambda^2 - \text{tr}(A)\lambda + \det(A) = 0\). Use the quadratic formula.

2
Solve for \(\lambda\)

The roots are the eigenvalues. A degree-\(n\) polynomial has \(n\) roots (possibly repeated, possibly complex).

3
For each \(\lambda_i\), find \(\text{null}(A - \lambda_i I)\)

Solve \((A-\lambda_i I)\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}\) by row reduction. The null space gives the eigenvectors for \(\lambda_i\).

📋 Full Worked Example
-- A = [[1,2],[2,1]] -- Step 1: det(A - λI) = det([[1-λ, 2],[2, 1-λ]]) = (1-λ)² - 4 = λ² - 2λ - 3 = (λ-3)(λ+1) = 0 Step 2: λ₁ = 3, λ₂ = -1 Step 3a: For λ₁=3: (A-3I)x = [[−2,2],[2,−2]]x = 0 → x₁ = x₂ → eigenvector: [1,1]ᵀ (or any multiple) Normalize: u₁ = [1,1]ᵀ/√2 Step 3b: For λ₂=-1: (A+I)x = [[2,2],[2,2]]x = 0 → x₁ = -x₂ → eigenvector: [-1,1]ᵀ Normalize: u₂ = [-1,1]ᵀ/√2 Verify: trace(A) = 2 = λ₁+λ₂ = 3+(-1) ✓ Verify: det(A) = -3 = λ₁·λ₂ = 3·(-1) ✓
🏛 Trace-Determinant Relations

For any \(n \times n\) matrix:

\[\text{tr}(A) = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i \qquad \det(A) = \prod_{i=1}^n \lambda_i\]

For 2×2: \(\lambda = \frac{\text{tr}(A) \pm \sqrt{\text{tr}(A)^2 - 4\det(A)}}{2}\)

4.4
Algebraic vs. Geometric Multiplicity
▾
📐 Two Kinds of Multiplicity

Algebraic multiplicity of \(\lambda_i\): the number of times \(\lambda_i\) appears as a root of \(\det(A-\lambda I)=0\).

Geometric multiplicity of \(\lambda_i\): the dimension of the null space of \((A - \lambda_i I)\) = number of linearly independent eigenvectors for \(\lambda_i\).

Always: geometric multiplicity ≤ algebraic multiplicity.

When geometric multiplicity < algebraic multiplicity for some eigenvalue, the matrix is not fully diagonalizable (it requires a Jordan form). For symmetric matrices, these are always equal — another reason symmetric matrices are so tractable.

📋 Example — Non-Diagonalizable Matrix
A = [[2, 1], Characteristic poly: (2-λ)² = 0 [0, 2]] So λ = 2 with algebraic multiplicity 2. (A - 2I) = [[0,1],[0,0]] Null space: x₂ = 0, x₁ free → only [1,0]ᵀ is an eigenvector Geometric multiplicity = 1 < 2 = algebraic multiplicity This matrix cannot be diagonalized! (It's in Jordan form already.)

Eigenvalue Decomposition (EVD)

Factoring a matrix into its eigenstructure — the master decomposition for square matrices

5.1
Derivation of EVD from First Principles
▾

The central idea: pack all the eigenvectors as columns of a matrix, and watch what happens when \(A\) multiplies it.

-- Let u₁,...,uₙ be eigenvectors with eigenvalues λ₁,...,λₙ -- -- Build the matrix U = [u₁ | u₂ | ... | uₙ] -- AU = A[u₁ | u₂ | ... | uₙ] = [Au₁ | Au₂ | ... | Auₙ] ← apply A to each column = [λ₁u₁ | λ₂u₂ | ... | λₙuₙ] ← eigenvector definition = [u₁|u₂|...|uₙ] · diag(λ₁,...,λₙ) ← factor out AU = UΛ where Λ = diag(λ₁,...,λₙ) If U is invertible: A = UΛU⁻¹ [Eigenvalue Decomposition] -- Or equivalently: U⁻¹AU = Λ [Diagonalization of A] --
📐 Eigenvalue Decomposition (EVD)
\[A = U\Lambda U^{-1}\]
  • \(U\): matrix whose columns are the eigenvectors of \(A\)
  • \(\Lambda = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)\): diagonal matrix of eigenvalues
  • Exists when \(A\) has \(n\) linearly independent eigenvectors
🏛 When Does EVD Exist?

Sufficient condition: If \(A\) has \(n\) distinct eigenvalues, it has \(n\) linearly independent eigenvectors → EVD exists.
Always: If \(A\) is symmetric (even with repeated eigenvalues), EVD exists with orthogonal \(U\) (Spectral Theorem).

5.2
Powers, Functions of Matrices & Eigenbasis Simplification
▾

Computing Matrix Powers

A^k = (UΛU⁻¹)^k = (UΛU⁻¹)(UΛU⁻¹)···(UΛU⁻¹) = U Λ (U⁻¹U) Λ (U⁻¹U) ··· Λ U⁻¹ = U Λ^k U⁻¹ -- Λ^k is trivial: diag(λ₁^k, λ₂^k, ..., λₙ^k) --

This turns an \(O(n^3)\) matrix multiply (done \(k\) times = \(O(kn^3)\)) into a single factorization (\(O(n^3)\) once) + cheap power computations (\(O(n)\)).

🧠 Eigenbasis Reduces Matrix Multiply to Scalar Multiply

This is the deepest insight of Module 5, from Lecture 6 Slides 62–63:

If you express \(\mathbf{x} = \sum_i \alpha_i \mathbf{u}_i\) in the eigenbasis, then:

\[A\mathbf{x} = \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i A\mathbf{u}_i = \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i \lambda_i \mathbf{u}_i\]

Instead of the expensive matrix-vector product \(A\mathbf{x}\), you just multiply each coordinate \(\alpha_i\) by the scalar \(\lambda_i\)! The eigenvalue is the "speed" at which component \(i\) grows under repeated application of \(A\).

📋 Using EVD to Compute A¹⁰
A = [[1,2],[2,1]], EVD: λ₁=3, u₁=[1,1]ᵀ/√2; λ₂=-1, u₂=[-1,1]ᵀ/√2 A^10 = U diag(3^10, (-1)^10) U⁻¹ = U diag(59049, 1) U⁻¹ -- Without EVD: 10 matrix multiplications, each O(n²) -- -- With EVD: just raise 3 and -1 to the 10th power --

Matrix Exponential \(e^{At}\)

Used in differential equations \(\frac{d\mathbf{u}}{dt} = A\mathbf{u}\). The solution is \(\mathbf{u}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{u}(0)\), where \(e^{At} = U e^{\Lambda t} U^{-1}\) and \(e^{\Lambda t} = \text{diag}(e^{\lambda_1 t}, \ldots, e^{\lambda_n t})\).

5.3
EVD for Symmetric Matrices — Spectral Decomposition
▾

For symmetric \(A = A^T\), the Spectral Theorem guarantees orthogonal eigenvectors. This means \(U^T = U^{-1}\), giving the spectral decomposition:

📐 Spectral Decomposition of Symmetric Matrix
\[A = Q\Lambda Q^T = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i\, \mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T\]

Each term \(\lambda_i \mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T\) is a rank-1 matrix — a projection onto the direction \(\mathbf{q}_i\), scaled by \(\lambda_i\). This decomposes \(A\) into \(n\) independent projections!

-- For A = [[1,2],[2,1]], eigenvectors u₁=[1,1]ᵀ/√2, u₂=[-1,1]ᵀ/√2 -- A = 3 · (1/√2)[1,1]ᵀ (1/√2)[1,1] + (-1) · (1/√2)[-1,1]ᵀ (1/√2)[-1,1] = 3 · [[0.5, 0.5], + (-1) · [[0.5, -0.5], [0.5, 0.5]] [-0.5, 0.5]] = [[1.5,1.5],[1.5,1.5]] + [[-0.5,0.5],[0.5,-0.5]] = [[1,2],[2,1]] ✓
5.4
Applications in Deep Learning
▾

Gradient Dynamics in RNNs

The gradient at time 0 involves \((W^T)^T\) applied \(T\) times. Controlled by eigenvalues of \(W\). Orthogonal RNNs constrain \(W\) to have all eigenvalues on the unit circle, maintaining stable gradients.

Spectral Normalization

Divide \(W\) by its spectral norm \(\sigma_1(W)\) (largest singular value). For symmetric \(W\), \(\sigma_1 = |\lambda_{\max}|\). Makes the Lipschitz constant of each layer = 1, stabilizing GAN training.

Hessian of Loss Functions

The Hessian \(H = \nabla^2\mathcal{L}\) is symmetric. Its eigenvalues describe the curvature: large eigenvalue = sharp curvature = hard to optimize. Adam adapts learning rates using approximate Hessian diagonal. The condition number \(\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}\) determines convergence speed of gradient descent.

Graph Neural Networks

Graph Laplacian \(L = D - A\) is symmetric PSD. Spectral graph convolutions are defined via its eigenvectors. The eigenvalues encode frequencies of the graph — similar to Fourier modes for signal processing.

Symmetric Matrices & the Spectral Theorem

The most important class of matrices in machine learning

6.1
The Spectral Theorem — Foundation of PCA
▾
🏛 Spectral Theorem (The Most Important Theorem in These Modules)

Every real symmetric matrix \(A = A^T \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}\) has:

  1. All real eigenvalues \(\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_n\)
  2. A complete set of orthonormal eigenvectors \(\mathbf{q}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{q}_n\): \(\mathbf{q}_i^T\mathbf{q}_j = \delta_{ij}\)
  3. Therefore: \(A = Q\Lambda Q^T\) where \(Q = [\mathbf{q}_1|\cdots|\mathbf{q}_n]\) is orthogonal

Let \(\mathbf{v}_i\) and \(\mathbf{v}_j\) be eigenvectors of symmetric \(A\) with \(\lambda_i \neq \lambda_j\). Consider:

\[\mathbf{v}_j^T A \mathbf{v}_i = \lambda_i (\mathbf{v}_j^T \mathbf{v}_i) \quad \text{(multiply eigenvector equation by } \mathbf{v}_j^T\text{)}\]

Since \(A = A^T\):

\[\mathbf{v}_j^T A \mathbf{v}_i = \mathbf{v}_j^T A^T \mathbf{v}_i = (A\mathbf{v}_j)^T \mathbf{v}_i = \lambda_j \mathbf{v}_j^T \mathbf{v}_i\]

So \(\lambda_i (\mathbf{v}_j^T\mathbf{v}_i) = \lambda_j(\mathbf{v}_j^T\mathbf{v}_i)\), giving \((\lambda_i - \lambda_j)(\mathbf{v}_j^T\mathbf{v}_i) = 0\).

Since \(\lambda_i \neq \lambda_j\), we must have \(\mathbf{v}_j^T\mathbf{v}_i = 0\). ∎

🧠 Why This Makes Symmetric Matrices Perfect for PCA

Because eigenvectors are orthonormal, finding coordinates is trivial: \(\alpha_i = \mathbf{q}_i^T\mathbf{x}\). And \(Q^{-1} = Q^T\) — the cheapest possible inverse. This means PCA can be computed efficiently: diagonalizing the covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) (which is symmetric) gives us an orthonormal basis where features are uncorrelated.

6.2
Quadratic Forms & the Rayleigh Quotient
▾
📐 Definition — Quadratic Form

For symmetric \(A\), the quadratic form is the scalar function \(f(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}\). It appears in: PCA variance formulas, ridge regression, Gaussian log-likelihoods, and Hessian-based curvature analysis.

🏛 Rayleigh Quotient Theorem — Lecture 6, Slide 26

For symmetric \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}\), the constrained optimization:

\[\max_{\mathbf{x}:\|\mathbf{x}\|=1} \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = \lambda_1 \quad \text{achieved at } \mathbf{x}^* = \mathbf{q}_1\] \[\min_{\mathbf{x}:\|\mathbf{x}\|=1} \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = \lambda_n \quad \text{achieved at } \mathbf{x}^* = \mathbf{q}_n\]

where \(\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_n\) are sorted eigenvalues.

Solve \(\max \mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}\) subject to \(\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x}=1\). Form the Lagrangian:

\[\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x},\lambda) = \mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} - \lambda(\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x}-1)\]

Set gradient to zero: \(\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = 2A\mathbf{x} - 2\lambda\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}\), giving \(A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}\).

So the critical points are eigenvectors of \(A\)! At eigenvector \(\mathbf{q}_i\): \(\mathbf{q}_i^T A \mathbf{q}_i = \lambda_i \mathbf{q}_i^T\mathbf{q}_i = \lambda_i\). The maximum is the largest eigenvalue. ∎

🧠 This IS PCA — Direction of Maximum Variance

PCA's first principal component is the direction \(\mathbf{p}\) that maximizes the variance of projected data. The variance in direction \(\mathbf{p}\) is \(\mathbf{p}^T\Sigma\mathbf{p}\) (subject to \(\|\mathbf{p}\|=1\)). By the Rayleigh Quotient Theorem, this maximum is \(\lambda_1\) achieved at the eigenvector for the largest eigenvalue of \(\Sigma\). PCA is literally just applying this theorem repeatedly.

6.3
Positive Semi-Definite (PSD) Matrices
▾
📐 Definition — Positive (Semi-)Definite

A symmetric matrix \(A\) is:

  • Positive Definite (PD): \(\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} > 0\) for all \(\mathbf{x}\neq\mathbf{0}\) ↔ all eigenvalues \(> 0\)
  • Positive Semi-Definite (PSD): \(\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} \geq 0\) for all \(\mathbf{x}\) ↔ all eigenvalues \(\geq 0\)
  • Indefinite: Some eigenvalues positive, some negative (saddle point geometry)
🏛 Gram Matrices Are Always PSD

For any matrix \(X\), the matrix \(A = X^TX\) is always symmetric PSD:

\[\mathbf{v}^T(X^TX)\mathbf{v} = (X\mathbf{v})^T(X\mathbf{v}) = \|X\mathbf{v}\|^2 \geq 0\]

Therefore covariance matrices \(\Sigma = \frac{1}{m}X^TX\) always have non-negative eigenvalues. This guarantees PCA variances \(\lambda_i/m \geq 0\).

Module 07

Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

Three interpretations of the same elegant idea — from IIT Lecture 6 Modules 6.4–6.7

7.1
Motivation — Why Reduce Dimensions?
▾

The curse of dimensionality: as dimensions grow, data becomes sparse, distances become meaningless, and computation explodes. PCA finds a lower-dimensional representation that preserves the most important structure.

📋 Redundant Dimension Example — Lecture 6 Slide 35

Consider a dataset with 3 features \(x, y, z\) where:

x y z 1.0 1.0 1.0 ← z ≈ y always! 0.5 0.0 0.0 0.25 1.0 1.0 0.35 1.5 1.5 0.45 1.0 1.0 0.57 2.0 2.1 ← high correlation ρ(y,z) ≈ 1 The 3rd feature adds almost ZERO new information. Drop z → lose almost nothing, save 33% of storage & computation.

The Pearson correlation \(\rho_{yz} \approx 1\) means \(z\) is nearly linearly dependent on \(y\). PCA formalizes this by finding the directions of maximum and minimum variance.

📐 PCA Goal

Find a new orthonormal basis \(\mathbf{p}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{p}_n\) such that when data is expressed in this basis, the new dimensions are:

  • Uncorrelated (off-diagonal covariances = 0)
  • Ordered by variance (\(\mathbf{p}_1\) captures most variance, then \(\mathbf{p}_2\), etc.)

Then we keep only the top \(k\) dimensions, discarding the rest.

7.2
Mathematical Setup — Data Matrix & Covariance
▾

Let \(X \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\) be a data matrix: \(m\) samples, \(n\) features. Assume columns are zero-mean (subtract mean of each feature).

📐 Covariance Matrix
\[\Sigma = \frac{1}{m} X^T X \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}\]

Entry \(\Sigma_{ij} = \frac{1}{m}\sum_k X_{ki}X_{kj}\) = sample covariance between features \(i\) and \(j\). The diagonal \(\Sigma_{ii}\) = variance of feature \(i\). Always symmetric PSD.

By definition, the covariance between features \(i\) and \(j\) is:

\[C_{ij} = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{k=1}^m (X_{ki} - \mu_i)(X_{kj} - \mu_j)\]

Since columns are zero-mean (\(\mu_i = 0\)):

\[C_{ij} = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{k=1}^m X_{ki}X_{kj} = \frac{1}{m}(X^TX)_{ij}\]

So \(\Sigma = \frac{1}{m}X^TX\). ∎

📐 Transformation to New Basis

Let \(P = [\mathbf{p}_1 | \cdots | \mathbf{p}_n]\) be an orthogonal matrix of new basis vectors. The transformed data matrix is:

\[\hat{X} = XP \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\]

Row \(i\) of \(\hat{X}\) is the representation of data point \(\mathbf{x}_i\) in the new basis: coordinate \(j\) is \(\hat{X}_{ij} = \mathbf{x}_i^T\mathbf{p}_j\) (projection of \(\mathbf{x}_i\) onto \(\mathbf{p}_j\)).

🏛 Key Theorem: Covariance of Transformed Data
\[\frac{1}{m}\hat{X}^T\hat{X} = \frac{1}{m}(XP)^T(XP) = P^T \frac{X^TX}{m} P = P^T\Sigma P\]

We want this to be diagonal: \(P^T\Sigma P = D\). Which orthogonal \(P\) diagonalizes \(\Sigma\)?

Answer: \(P = Q\), the matrix of eigenvectors of \(\Sigma\). By the Spectral Theorem, \(Q^T\Sigma Q = \Lambda\).

7.3
Interpretation 1 — Decorrelation (Zero Covariance)
▾

We want the transformed dimensions to be uncorrelated. Uncorrelated means the off-diagonal entries of the transformed covariance matrix are zero — i.e., the covariance matrix is diagonal.

-- We want: -- (1/m)·X̂ᵀX̂ = PᵀΣP = D (diagonal) -- Which P diagonalizes Σ? -- P = eigenvectors of Σ = (1/m)XᵀX -- Then: PᵀΣP = QᵀΛQ·Q = QᵀQΛ = Λ (diagonal of eigenvalues) -- -- The eigenvalues appear on the diagonal = variances of new dimensions! --
🧠 Decorrelation = Diagonalizing the Covariance Matrix

In the original basis, features are correlated (off-diagonal \(\Sigma_{ij} \neq 0\)). In the eigenvector basis, features are perfectly uncorrelated (\(\hat{\Sigma}_{ij} = 0\) for \(i\neq j\)). Uncorrelated features are easier to learn from — they don't confound each other.

Batch normalization does something similar: it normalizes activations to have zero mean and unit variance, reducing internal covariate shift.

7.4
Interpretation 2 — Minimum Reconstruction Error
▾

We have \(n\) basis vectors \(\mathbf{p}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{p}_n\). We keep only the top \(k\). We want to minimize the total squared reconstruction error when we discard dimensions \(k+1, \ldots, n\):

📐 Reconstruction Error
\[e = \sum_{i=1}^m \|\mathbf{x}_i - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_i\|^2 \quad \text{where } \hat{\mathbf{x}}_i = \sum_{j=1}^k (\mathbf{p}_j^T\mathbf{x}_i)\,\mathbf{p}_j\]

This simplifies to:

\[e = \sum_{j=k+1}^n \mathbf{p}_j^T (mC)\mathbf{p}_j = m\sum_{j=k+1}^n \mathbf{p}_j^T\Sigma\mathbf{p}_j\]

We minimize this subject to \(\mathbf{p}_j^T\mathbf{p}_j = 1\). By the Rayleigh Quotient Theorem, the minimum is achieved when \(\mathbf{p}_j\) are eigenvectors of \(\Sigma\) for the smallest eigenvalues.

📋 Concrete Example — Lecture 6 Slide 50
Data: two new basis vectors u₁=[1,1]ᵀ/√2, u₂=[-1,1]ᵀ/√2 (eigenvectors) Point x = [3.3, 3.0]ᵀ α₁ = u₁ᵀx = (3.3+3.0)/√2 = 6.3/√2 ≈ 4.455 α₂ = u₂ᵀx = (-3.3+3.0)/√2 = -0.3/√2 ≈ -0.212 Perfect reconstruction (k=2): x = α₁u₁ + α₂u₂ = [3.3, 3.0]ᵀ ✓ Compressed (k=1, drop u₂): x̂ = α₁u₁ = 4.455·[1/√2, 1/√2]ᵀ = [3.15, 3.15]ᵀ Reconstruction error = ||[3.3,3.0] - [3.15,3.15]||² = 0.0225+0.0225 = 0.045 -- The small error (0.045) is due to the tiny α₂ (low variance direction) --
7.5
Interpretation 3 — Maximum Variance Directions
▾
🏛 Variance Along the i-th Principal Component

The variance of the projected data along the \(i\)-th eigenvector \(\mathbf{p}_i\) of \(\Sigma\) is:

\[\frac{\hat{X}_i^T\hat{X}_i}{m} = \frac{1}{m}(X\mathbf{p}_i)^T(X\mathbf{p}_i) = \mathbf{p}_i^T\frac{X^TX}{m}\mathbf{p}_i = \mathbf{p}_i^T\Sigma\mathbf{p}_i = \mathbf{p}_i^T(\lambda_i\mathbf{p}_i) = \lambda_i\]

So variance along the \(i\)-th PC = \(\lambda_i\) (or \(\lambda_i/m\) depending on normalisation). Larger eigenvalue = more variance = more information!

🧠 Three Interpretations, One Answer

All three interpretations (decorrelation, min error, max variance) lead to the same solution: the eigenvectors of \(\Sigma\), sorted by decreasing eigenvalue. This is not a coincidence — they're all asking the same geometric question in different language. The eigenvectors of the covariance matrix are the "natural axes" of the data.

▾
1
Center the data

Subtract the column mean from each feature: \(X \leftarrow X - \mathbf{1}\boldsymbol{\mu}^T\). Ensures \(\Sigma = \frac{1}{m}X^TX\) exactly.

2
Compute covariance \(\Sigma = \frac{1}{m}X^TX \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}\)

If \(n\) is huge (e.g., images), this is expensive. Use \(XX^T \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times m}\) + kernel trick, or directly use SVD of \(X\).

3
Eigendecompose \(\Sigma = Q\Lambda Q^T\)

Sort eigenvectors by decreasing eigenvalue: \(\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_n \geq 0\). The eigenvectors \(\mathbf{q}_i\) are the principal components.

4

Pick \(k\) such that \(\sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i / \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i \geq 0.95\) (95% explained variance). Or use a scree plot (elbow method).

5
Project: \(\hat{X} = XQ_k\) where \(Q_k = [\mathbf{q}_1|\cdots|\mathbf{q}_k]\)

Each row of \(\hat{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times k}\) is the low-dimensional representation of one data point.

6
Optional: reconstruct for visualization

\(\tilde{X} = \hat{X}Q_k^T \approx X\). The reconstruction error \(\|X - \tilde{X}\|_F^2 = m\sum_{i=k+1}^n \lambda_i\).

Module 08

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

The generalization of EVD to every matrix — the most powerful factorization in linear algebra

8.1
Why EVD Isn't Enough — The Need for SVD
▾

EVD requires square matrices. But neural network weight matrices are rectangular: \(W \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{out}\times d_{in}}\). And eigenvectors require \(A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}\) — but if the input and output live in different spaces, this equation makes no sense dimensionally.

A ∈ ℝ^(m×n): A · x = result (m×n)(n×1) = (m×1) For eigenvector: need Ax = λx → LHS ∈ ℝᵐ, RHS ∈ ℝⁿ If m ≠ n: IMPOSSIBLE. Eigenvectors can't exist for rectangular A.

But we still want the benefit of EVD: expressing the transformation in a "natural basis" where it acts diagonally. SVD provides exactly this, using two separate orthonormal bases — one for the input space, one for the output space.

8.2
The Big Idea — Paired Singular Vectors
▾

Instead of one set of eigenvectors, we find pairs of vectors \((\mathbf{v}_i, \mathbf{u}_i)\) such that:

\[A\mathbf{v}_i = \sigma_i\mathbf{u}_i\] where \(\mathbf{v}_i \in \mathbb{R}^n\) (input space), \(\mathbf{u}_i \in \mathbb{R}^m\) (output space), and \(\sigma_i \geq 0\) (singular value).
🧠 Geometric Picture

Think of \(A\) as a function from \(\mathbb{R}^n\) to \(\mathbb{R}^m\). SVD finds the best "input-output axis pairs":

  • \(\mathbf{v}_i\): the \(i\)-th input direction
  • \(\mathbf{u}_i\): the \(i\)-th output direction that \(\mathbf{v}_i\) maps to
  • \(\sigma_i\): how much the \(i\)-th input axis is stretched

In this pair-basis, \(A\) acts diagonally: it maps \(\mathbf{v}_i\) to \(\sigma_i\mathbf{u}_i\). Every input \(\mathbf{x} = \sum_i \alpha_i\mathbf{v}_i\) maps to \(A\mathbf{x} = \sum_i \alpha_i\sigma_i\mathbf{u}_i\).

📐 Singular Value Decomposition
\[\boxed{A = U\Sigma V^T}, \qquad A \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\]
  • \(U \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times m}\): orthogonal matrix, columns = left singular vectors (output basis)
  • \(\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\): diagonal with \(\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_k \geq 0\), \(k=\min(m,n)\) (singular values)
  • \(V \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}\): orthogonal matrix, columns = right singular vectors (input basis)

SVD exists for every real matrix \(A\). No exceptions.

8.3
Derivation — How to Find U, Σ, V
▾

The key trick: form the symmetric matrices \(A^TA\) and \(AA^T\), then use EVD on them.

-- If A = UΣVᵀ, then: -- AᵀA = (UΣVᵀ)ᵀ(UΣVᵀ) = VΣᵀUᵀ·UΣVᵀ = VΣᵀΣVᵀ = V · diag(σ₁², σ₂², ...) · Vᵀ → Eigenvalues of AᵀA are σᵢ² → Eigenvectors of AᵀA are the RIGHT singular vectors vᵢ AAᵀ = (UΣVᵀ)(UΣVᵀ)ᵀ = UΣVᵀ·VΣᵀUᵀ = UΣΣᵀUᵀ = U · diag(σ₁², σ₂², ...) · Uᵀ → Eigenvectors of AAᵀ are the LEFT singular vectors uᵢ -- Singular values: σᵢ = √(λᵢ(AᵀA)) -- -- Note: AᵀA and AAᵀ have the same nonzero eigenvalues! --
🏛 Key Connection: SVD ↔ EVD
  • V = eigenvectors of \(A^TA\) (right singular vectors)
  • U = eigenvectors of \(AA^T\) (left singular vectors)
  • Singular values \(\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i(A^TA)}\)
  • Nonzero eigenvalues of \(A^TA\) = nonzero eigenvalues of \(AA^T\)
  • Eigenvectors of \(\Sigma = \frac{1}{m}X^TX\) = right singular vectors of \(X\)

Low-Rank (Truncated) SVD

A = U Σ Vᵀ = Σᵢ₌₁ᵣ σᵢ uᵢ vᵢᵀ -- Each term σᵢuᵢvᵢᵀ is a rank-1 matrix (outer product) -- -- σ₁ ≥ σ₂ ≥ ... ≥ σᵣ > 0 -- Rank-k approximation: Aₖ = Σᵢ₌₁ᵏ σᵢ uᵢ vᵢᵀ This is the BEST possible rank-k approximation of A!

\[A_k = \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i\,\mathbf{u}_i\mathbf{v}_i^T = \underset{\text{rank}(B)\leq k}{\arg\min}\|A - B\|_F^2\]

\[\|A - A_k\|_F^2 = \sum_{i=k+1}^r \sigma_i^2\]

Discarding the smallest singular values causes the least damage.

8.5
Deep Learning Applications of SVD
▾

LoRA — Low-Rank Adaptation

Fine-tune large LLMs by constraining weight updates: \(\Delta W = BA\) where \(B \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times r}\), \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{r\times d}\), \(r \ll d\). Instead of \(d^2\) parameters, only \(2dr\). SVD shows this is optimal for low-rank approximations.

Spectral Normalization

Normalize each weight matrix by its largest singular value: \(\tilde{W} = W/\sigma_1(W)\). Ensures each layer has Lipschitz constant 1. Stabilizes GAN training by preventing discriminator from growing unboundedly.

Matrix Factorization for Recommendations

User-item matrix \(R \approx U\Sigma V^T\). Users \(= U\Sigma\), items \(= V\Sigma\). The \(k\)-dimensional user/item embeddings come from the top-\(k\) singular vectors. This is collaborative filtering via SVD.

Orthogonal Weight Initialization

Sample random weight matrix, compute \(A = U\Sigma V^T\), return \(U\) (or \(V\)). This gives a perfectly orthogonal initialization — no vanishing/exploding gradients at startup because orthogonal matrices preserve vector norms.

PCA via SVD of X

Instead of computing \(X^TX\) (expensive if \(n\) is huge), directly compute the SVD of \(X\). Right singular vectors \(= \) eigenvectors of \(X^TX\). Numerically more stable and often faster.

Data Compression & Image Reconstruction

Use Eckart-Young: keep top \(k\) singular values. Image quality degrades gracefully as \(k\) decreases. Useful for understanding model compression via low-rank weight matrices.

T1
Theorem: Eigenvectors with Distinct Eigenvalues Are Linearly Independent
▾
🏛 Statement

If \(\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\) are eigenvectors of \(A\) with distinct eigenvalues \(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k\), they are linearly independent.

Proof by induction on \(k\):

Base case \(k=1\): A single nonzero eigenvector is linearly independent. ✓

Inductive step: Assume the first \(k-1\) eigenvectors are independent. Suppose for contradiction:

\[c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_k\mathbf{v}_k = \mathbf{0} \quad \cdots (1)\]

Apply \(A\) to both sides:

\[c_1\lambda_1\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\lambda_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_k\lambda_k\mathbf{v}_k = \mathbf{0} \quad \cdots (2)\]

Multiply (1) by \(\lambda_k\) and subtract from (2):

\[c_1(\lambda_1-\lambda_k)\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2(\lambda_2-\lambda_k)\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_{k-1}(\lambda_{k-1}-\lambda_k)\mathbf{v}_{k-1} = \mathbf{0}\]

Since eigenvalues are distinct, \((\lambda_i - \lambda_k) \neq 0\) for \(i < k\). By the inductive hypothesis, \(\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_{k-1}\) are independent, so \(c_i(\lambda_i-\lambda_k) = 0 \Rightarrow c_i = 0\) for all \(i < k\). Substituting back into (1): \(c_k\mathbf{v}_k = \mathbf{0}\), and since \(\mathbf{v}_k \neq \mathbf{0}\), we get \(c_k = 0\). All coefficients are 0. ∎

T2
Theorem: Symmetric Matrices Have Real Eigenvalues
▾
🏛 Statement

All eigenvalues of a real symmetric matrix are real.

Suppose \(A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}\) where \(\lambda\) might be complex, \(\mathbf{x}\) might be complex. Take the conjugate transpose (denoted \(^*\)) of both sides:

\[\mathbf{x}^* A^T = \bar{\lambda} \mathbf{x}^*\]

Since \(A = A^T\), multiply on the right by \(\mathbf{x}\):

\[\mathbf{x}^* A \mathbf{x} = \bar{\lambda} \mathbf{x}^*\mathbf{x}\]

But also, multiplying \(A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}\) on the left by \(\mathbf{x}^*\):

\[\mathbf{x}^* A \mathbf{x} = \lambda \mathbf{x}^*\mathbf{x}\]

Subtracting: \((\lambda - \bar{\lambda})\mathbf{x}^*\mathbf{x} = 0\). Since \(\mathbf{x}^*\mathbf{x} = \|\mathbf{x}\|^2 > 0\), we get \(\lambda = \bar{\lambda}\), meaning \(\lambda\) is real. ∎

T3
Theorem: Spectral Theorem (Symmetric EVD)
▾
🏛 Statement

Every real symmetric \(A\) has a complete orthonormal set of eigenvectors: \(A = Q\Lambda Q^T\) with \(Q^TQ = I\).

Sketch (induction on \(n\)):

Base case \(n=1\): trivial. For the inductive step: \(A\) has at least one real eigenvalue \(\lambda_1\) (by the fundamental theorem of algebra, since eigenvalues are real for symmetric matrices). Let \(\mathbf{q}_1\) be the corresponding unit eigenvector.

Consider the subspace \(W = \{\mathbf{q}_1\}^\perp\) (all vectors orthogonal to \(\mathbf{q}_1\)). For any \(\mathbf{w} \in W\): \(\mathbf{q}_1^T(A\mathbf{w}) = (A\mathbf{q}_1)^T\mathbf{w} = \lambda_1\mathbf{q}_1^T\mathbf{w} = 0\), so \(A\mathbf{w} \in W\). Thus \(A\) maps \(W\) to itself — we can restrict \(A\) to \(W\).

The restricted map \(A|_W : W \to W\) is also symmetric and has dimension \(n-1\). By induction, it has an orthonormal eigenbasis \(\mathbf{q}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{q}_n\) for \(W\). Together with \(\mathbf{q}_1\), these form an orthonormal eigenbasis for all of \(\mathbb{R}^n\). ∎

T4
Theorem: SVD Exists for Every Matrix
▾
🏛 Statement

Every \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\) has a singular value decomposition \(A = U\Sigma V^T\).

\(A^TA\) is symmetric PSD (proof: \(\mathbf{v}^T A^TA \mathbf{v} = \|A\mathbf{v}\|^2 \geq 0\)). By the Spectral Theorem, it has a real orthonormal eigenbasis \(\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n\) with eigenvalues \(\lambda_1 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_n \geq 0\).

Let \(r = \text{rank}(A)\). The first \(r\) eigenvalues are positive; the rest are 0. Define \(\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}\) for \(i=1,\ldots,r\). Define \(\mathbf{u}_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i}A\mathbf{v}_i\) for \(i=1,\ldots,r\).

Check: \(\mathbf{u}_i^T\mathbf{u}_j = \frac{1}{\sigma_i\sigma_j}\mathbf{v}_i^T A^TA \mathbf{v}_j = \frac{\lambda_j}{\sigma_i\sigma_j}\mathbf{v}_i^T\mathbf{v}_j = \delta_{ij}\). So \(\mathbf{u}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{u}_r\) are orthonormal.

Extend to an orthonormal basis \(\mathbf{u}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{u}_m\) of \(\mathbb{R}^m\) (Gram-Schmidt). Then \(AV = U\Sigma\) (verified by checking each column), so \(A = U\Sigma V^T\). ∎

T5
Theorem: Eckart–Young (Best Low-Rank Approximation)
▾
🏛 Statement

Among all rank-\(k\) matrices \(B\), the one minimizing \(\|A - B\|_F^2\) is \(A_k = \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i\mathbf{u}_i\mathbf{v}_i^T\), with error \(\|A - A_k\|_F^2 = \sum_{i=k+1}^r \sigma_i^2\).

Error computation: \(\|A - A_k\|_F^2 = \|U\Sigma V^T - U\Sigma_k V^T\|_F^2 = \|U(\Sigma-\Sigma_k)V^T\|_F^2\). Since \(U, V\) are orthogonal and the Frobenius norm is invariant under orthogonal multiplication: \(= \|\Sigma-\Sigma_k\|_F^2 = \sum_{i=k+1}^r \sigma_i^2\).

Optimality (sketch): Let \(B\) be any rank-\(k\) matrix. By the min-max characterization of singular values, \(\sigma_{k+1}(A) = \min_{\text{rank}(C)\leq k} \|A-C\|_2\). More generally, a dimension argument shows that \(\|A-B\|_F \geq \|A-A_k\|_F\) for all rank-\(k\) \(B\). (Full proof uses the unitary invariance of the Frobenius norm and the Courant-Fischer min-max theorem.) ∎

T6
Theorem: Rayleigh Quotient (Max Variance Direction)
▾
🏛 Statement

For symmetric \(A\): \(\max_{\|\mathbf{x}\|=1} \mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} = \lambda_{\max}(A)\), achieved at the corresponding eigenvector.

Express \(\mathbf{x}\) in the eigenbasis: \(\mathbf{x} = \sum_i c_i\mathbf{q}_i\) with \(\sum_i c_i^2 = 1\) (since eigenvectors are orthonormal).

\[\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} = \left(\sum_i c_i\mathbf{q}_i\right)^T A \left(\sum_j c_j\mathbf{q}_j\right) = \sum_{i,j} c_ic_j \mathbf{q}_i^T(A\mathbf{q}_j) = \sum_i c_i^2 \lambda_i\]

(Cross terms vanish by orthogonality: \(\mathbf{q}_i^T A\mathbf{q}_j = \lambda_j\mathbf{q}_i^T\mathbf{q}_j = 0\) for \(i\neq j\).)

\[\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} = \sum_i c_i^2 \lambda_i \leq \lambda_{\max}\sum_i c_i^2 = \lambda_{\max}\]

Equality holds when \(\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{q}_{\max}\) (the eigenvector for \(\lambda_{\max}\)). ∎

The Grand Summary

  • A matrix is a linear transformation: it stretches, rotates, and reflects vectors
  • Eigenvectors are the "natural axes" of a transformation — they only scale
  • EVD \(A=U\Lambda U^{-1}\) diagonalizes \(A\); matrix powers become trivial scalar powers
  • Symmetric matrices always have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors
  • The Spectral Theorem: \(A=Q\Lambda Q^T\) — the backbone of PCA
  • PCA finds eigenvectors of \(\Sigma=\frac{1}{m}X^TX\): the axes of maximum variance
  • SVD \(A=U\Sigma V^T\) works for any matrix; expresses as rotation-scale-rotation
  • Eckart-Young: top-\(k\) singular values/vectors give the best rank-\(k\) approximation
  • Singular values = \(\sqrt{\text{eigenvalues of }A^TA}\); SVD = generalized EVD
  • Eigenvalue magnitude controls gradient stability in deep networks (explode/vanish/stable)
  • Covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) is always symmetric PSD: eigenvalues \(\geq 0\)
  • Orthogonal matrices preserve lengths; they're the perfect change-of-basis matrices
